Meta Ads Surge — fitness app Spec project
Problem

Installs are cheap, but most users quit before finishing one full workout.

Goal

Find which objection is actually stopping sign-ups.

Strategy

Test three angles against the same offer: identity, proof, and simplicity.

Angle: Identity

You're not "getting into fitness." You're already the kind of person who shows up.

Surge builds the workout around who you already are — not who an influencer thinks you should become.

Angle: Social proof

40,000 people did their first Surge workout this week. None of them "had time" either.

12-minute sessions. No equipment required. Start today, free.

Angle: Simplicity

One app. One plan. Zero decisions before 7am.

Open the app, press play, done. Surge picks the workout so you don't have to.

Strategy note

Three ads, three entry points, same offer. Testing identity, proof, and simplicity against each other shows which objection is actually blocking sign-ups instead of guessing.

Target outcomeLower cost-per-trial by isolating the highest-converting angle.
Brand Messaging Dusk — sleep & wellness supplement Spec project
Problem

The sleep-supplement category is full of vague "calm & rest" claims that mean nothing on shelf.

Goal

Give Dusk one claim people can remember, repeat, and trust.

Strategy

Replace vague wellness language with one concrete, testable promise.

Fall asleep before your phone does.

Dusk is designed to work with your evening, not against it — no vague "calm" claims, just a fast-acting formula with every ingredient and dose listed on the label.

Fast-acting

"Kicks in before your evening scroll ends."

Non-habit forming

"No groggy mornings. No dependency."

Transparent dosing

"Every ingredient, every milligram, on the label."

Strategy note

"Wellness" is the most overused word in the category and the least trusted. Naming a concrete, felt moment — falling asleep before your phone does — gives the brand a claim competitors can't copy without sounding derivative.

Target outcomeStronger shelf/ad differentiation and higher message recall vs. generic wellness claims.
Landing Page Loop — habit-tracking app Spec project
Problem

Users build a 5-day streak, miss one day, and never open the app again.

Goal

Improve Day-30 retention among users who break an early streak.

Strategy

Reframe restarting as normal instead of failure.

Start again. Then again. Then again — until it sticks.

Loop doesn't punish you for breaking a streak. It just asks what you're doing tomorrow. Track the habits that matter, see your pattern instead of your perfection, and build a routine that survives a bad week.

Start your loop — free for 14 days →
Strategy note

Streak-based apps make one missed day feel like failure, which is exactly when people delete the app. Making the restart the whole point removes the shame that causes silent churn.

Target outcomeFewer "one missed day" uninstalls; higher week-4 open rate.
Email Campaign Flowbase — project management SaaS Spec project
Problem

Trial users set up a workspace, go quiet, and let the trial lapse before converting.

Goal

Get inactive trial users to complete one activating action before expiry.

Strategy

Point to the one unfinished step instead of a generic "trial ending" nudge.

Subject line

Your Flowbase trial ends Friday — one thing's still unfinished.

You set up your workspace but never invited your team — which is usually where Flowbase actually starts saving people time. Add two teammates before Friday and we'll extend your trial a week, on us.

Invite your team →
Strategy note

Most trial-ending emails lead with urgency alone, which doesn't fix the real problem: the user never reached the feature that proves value. Naming the exact unfinished step turns a countdown email into a product nudge.

Target outcomeHigher trial-to-paid conversion via team-invite activation.
How I Work

Clients don't buy copy. They buy the thinking behind it.

Every project above followed the same process — the words are just the visible last step.

01
Research

Product, competitors, and existing reviews or feedback — find out what's already true before writing anything.

02
Find the objection

Every drop-off has a real reason: doubt, confusion, timing, price. Name it before trying to solve it.

03
Pick one message

One clear angle beats five decent ones. Everything else gets cut.

04
Write & test

Draft, tighten, and where possible, test variants against each other instead of guessing which one wins.

About

If your ads, page, or emails aren't converting, it's usually the message — not the offer

Most SaaS and wellness brands come to copy last — after the product's built, the ad budget is set, and the page is already live — and then wonder why traffic isn't turning into customers. Nine times out of ten, the problem isn't the offer. It's that the copy is describing features while the reader is still stuck on one objection.

That's the problem I work on: finding the actual objection — doubt, confusion, timing, price — and writing the ad, page, or email around that instead of around the product spec sheet.
